Context – WEC‑Pro

Renewable Energy, Inclusive Growth — and Why Wave Energy Matters Now

From resource constraints to resilient, community‑powered systems across Europe

1) Why the energy transition is no longer optional

The IPCC’s AR6 Synthesis Report finds human‑driven warming is triggering widespread impacts, and keeping 1.5–2 °C within reach requires very rapid, deep emission cuts across all sectors.

The IEA’s World Energy Outlook 2024 reports renewables generated ~30% of global electricity in 2023 while fossil fuels’ share of power fell to ~60%—its lowest in 50 years; yet fossil fuels still met about 80% of total energy demand.

The UN projects world population at 9.7 billion by 2050 and notes the urban share will rise to ~68% by mid‑century—putting pressure on infrastructure, energy access and equity.

2) From “growth at all costs” to well‑being and resilience

Policy and market signals are shifting from pure GDP growth to resilience, human development and environmental integrity. The World Bank Group moved to Paris‑align all new operations from 1 July 2023, signaling that public finance should avoid carbon lock‑in and support low‑carbon pathways.

What this means in practice: prioritize efficiency, electrification, clean power, and distributed systems that bring benefits closer to people and reduce systemic risk.

3) Decentralization and energy communities: from idea to EU law

The Clean Energy Package created Renewable Energy Communities (RECs) and Citizen Energy Communities (CECs), and the RED III (2023) strengthened the EU’s overall 2030 renewables target (to 45% share of final energy) and accelerated permitting—key enablers for local, citizen‑owned projects.

Why it matters: community ownership can keep value local, ease grid integration through flexibility and demand response, and build public support for new infrastructure.

4) Marine renewables: the strategic gap‑filler

Europe’s Offshore Renewable Energy Strategy (2020) sets a trajectory for ocean energy (wave & tidal)—with 1 GW by 2030 and ~40 GW by 2050 flagged as realistic ambitions.

Ocean Energy Europe’s 2024 Stats & Trends charts full‑scale wave devices in the water, a 165 MW pre‑commercial pipeline across 15 farms supported by public funding, and rising private investment.

Why wave energy in particular?

  • Complementarity & stability: Wave resources often lag wind conditions and can smooth output when winds subside.
  • Seasonal match: Wave energy is strongest in winter, when demand and system stress are highest.
  • Grid & infrastructure synergies: Shared offshore platforms, cables and ports with floating wind can lower levelized costs.

5) WEC‑Pro’s position: making wave energy bankable and socially valuable

Our thesis: Wave energy can stabilize variable fleets, localize value through coastal supply chains, and coexist with other ocean uses.

  • Technology maturation & operations: validating survivability, controls, and maintainability; quantifying LCOE glide paths.
  • System integration: documenting wind–wave complementarity and shared infrastructure models.
  • Community value: aligning with EU REC/CEC frameworks to share ownership and benefits.

6) Priorities for 2025–2030

  1. Scale pilots to pre‑commercial arrays (10–30 MW), using revenue‑stabilization tools.
  2. Exploit hybridization with floating wind to share infrastructure.
  3. Embed energy communities in coastal deployment models.
  4. Leverage EU policy tailwinds—RED III and REPowerEU.
